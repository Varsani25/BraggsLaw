Bragg's Law

Aim :

The aim of Bragg's law is to determine the spacing between the crystal planes in a crystal lattice by analyzing the diffraction pattern of X-rays that are incident on the crystal at a particular angle

Appratus :

Bragg's law is a fundamental principle in X-ray crystallography, which describes the relationship between the angles of incidence and diffraction of X-rays by a crystal lattice. The apparatus required to demonstrate Bragg's law is relatively simple and typically includes the following:

(1) X-ray source (such as a tube) : A source of X-rays, such as an X-ray tube, is needed to produce a beam of X-rays.

(2) Crystal sample: A crystal of a known structure is required to diffract the X-ray beam. The crystal should be of a suitable size and orientation to produce a diffracted beam that can be measured.

(3) Collimator (to control the beam of X-rays) : A collimator is used to narrow the X-ray beam and ensure that it is of a suitable size and shape to interact with the crystal sample.

(4) X-ray detector (such as a film or digital sensor) : A detector is used to measure the intensity and angle of the diffracted X-ray beam. Commonly used detectors include photographic film, scintillation counters, and area detectors.

(5) Goniometer (to rotate the sample) : A goniometer is used to position the crystal sample and detector at the correct angles with respect to the X-ray beam. This allows the diffraction pattern to be measured at different angles.

(6) Data analysis software: Specialized software is used to analyze the diffraction data and calculate the crystal structure.

Together, these components make up a basic X-ray diffractometer, which is the primary tool used for crystallographic research and the determination of crystal structures using Bragg's law.

Derivation :

(Deriving Bragg's Law by Paul Schields)

  • Bragg's Law can easily be derived by considering the conditions necessary to make the phases of the beams coincide when the incident angle equals and reflecting angle.

  • The rays of the incident beam are always in phase and parallel up to the point at which the top beam strikes the top layer at atom z (Fig. 1).

  • The second beam continues to the next layer where it is scattered by atom B. The second beam must travel the extra distance AB + BC if the two beams are to continue traveling adjacent and parallel.

  • This extra distance must be an integral (n) multiple of the wavelength (λ) for the phases of the two beams to be the same:


‣ Fig. 1 Deriving Bragg's Law using the reflection geometry and applying trigonometry. The lower beam must travel the extra distance (AB + BC) to continue traveling parallel and adjacent to the top beam.

‣ Recognizing d as the hypotenuse of the right triangle Abz, we can use trigonometry to relate d and Θ to the distance (AB + BC). The distance AB is opposite Θ so,

nλ = AB +BC (2).

AB = d sinΘ(3).  Because AB = BC eq. (2) becomes,

nλ = 2AB (4).  Substituting eq. (3) in eq. (4) we have,

nλ = 2 d sinΘ

and Bragg's Law has been derived. The location of the surface does not change the derivation of Bragg's Law.


Instrument :


Steps :

  1. Mount the crystal sample on the goniometer in the X-ray beam path. The crystal should be positioned so that the X-rays hit the crystal at an appropriate angle.
  2. Use the collimator to adjust the size and shape of the X-ray beam.
  3. Turn on the X-ray source and direct the beam at the crystal sample.
  4. Rotate the goniometer to change the angle between the crystal and the incoming X-rays. This will change the angle of diffraction.
  5. Place the X-ray detector on the other side of the crystal and position it to detect the diffracted X-rays.
  6. Record the diffraction pattern by either taking a photograph of the detector or recording the signal digitally.
  7. Analyze the diffraction pattern to determine the angles of the diffracted X-rays and calculate the interplanar spacing of the crystal lattice using Bragg's law.

Note: The specific details of this procedure may vary depending on the specific experiment and equipment used. It is important to consult the instructions provided with your equipment and follow established laboratory safety protocols.



In an XRD spectrometer, a sample is exposed to a beam of X-rays, which causes the atoms in the sample to diffract the X-rays in different directions. The diffracted X-rays are collected by a detector, and the resulting diffraction pattern is analyzed to determine the crystal structure of the material.

The diffraction pattern is produced when the X-rays interact with the atoms in the sample in a way that depends on the spacing of the atoms in the crystal lattice. The X-rays that are diffracted by the atoms interfere with each other, resulting in a pattern of bright spots and dark areas on the detector. The positions and intensities of these spots are determined by the crystal structure of the material.

XRD spectrometers are used in a wide range of applications, including material science, chemistry, physics, and geology. They are capable of providing information about the crystalline structure of materials, including the lattice constant, crystal symmetry, and orientation of the crystal lattice. This information can be used to determine the composition of a material, to identify unknown materials, and to study the properties of materials at the atomic and molecular level.
